American's Views on Fake News

By Rachael Tonietto

American's Confusion due to Fake News

In light of the 2016 election, many Americans have become concerned that ersatz information is taking a toll on politics. As social media grows more vital as a source for political news, so does the fear that false information is being disseminated out to the public. Research gathered by the Pew Research Center shows that Americans suspect fake news is having an inimical impact on innocent individuals seeking the facts. A new survey of 1,002 U.S. adults released by the PRC found that two-in-three adults feel fabricated news stories cause unwarranted confusion about current issues and events. Whether intentionally or accidentally, fake news has been widely shared and a concern about this dissemination is present amongst the hoi-polloi from various economic, educational, partisan and other demographic backgrounds.

American's Confidence in Deciphering Fake News

Despite the great amount of confusion 64% of Americans believe ersatz news is causing, four-in-ten American's still feel confident in their ability to decipher false information from the facts, and 49% feel somewhat confident in their faculties. Given the circumstances, it is difficult to measure the precise number of American's capable of deciphering fake stories from factual ones, for they could easily confuse the two and not recognize the mistake. However, 32% of Americans say they often come across fabricated news stories online and are able to recogonize them as such; regardless of this claims accuracy, these figures still present a sense of the public's perception in the matter.
